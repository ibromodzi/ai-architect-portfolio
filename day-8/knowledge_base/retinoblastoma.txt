Retinoblastoma

Retinoblastoma is a rare type of eye cancer that develops in the retina, the light-sensitive tissue at the back of the eye. It is the most common eye cancer in children and almost always occurs in children under five years of age. It can affect one eye (unilateral) or both eyes (bilateral). Bilateral cases are more likely to be hereditary.

The most recognisable early sign of retinoblastoma is leukocoria, often described as a white or cat's eye reflex visible in the pupil — particularly noticeable in photographs taken with a flash, where one eye reflects red and the affected eye appears white. Other signs may include a noticeable outward or inward turning of one eye (strabismus), redness or swelling of the eye, and reduced or changed vision. Because young children cannot communicate vision changes, parents and caregivers play an important role in noticing these signs.

Retinoblastoma originates from a mutation in the RB1 gene, which normally helps regulate cell growth. In hereditary retinoblastoma, this mutation is present in all cells of the body and can be passed from parent to child. Children from families with a known history of retinoblastoma may be recommended for regular screening examinations even before birth. In non-hereditary cases, the mutation occurs only in the retinal cells.

Outcomes for children with retinoblastoma are generally good when the condition is detected and treated early. Treatment options depend on the size, location, and stage of the tumour, and may include laser therapy, cryotherapy, chemotherapy, radiation, or in some cases removal of the eye (enucleation) to prevent the spread of cancer. Advances in treatment have significantly improved survival rates, particularly in higher-income settings.

Early detection is critical. Parents who notice a white pupil reflex, unusual eye alignment, or any other eye change in their child should seek urgent assessment from a healthcare professional. In many African countries, awareness campaigns are actively working to improve early presentation and treatment access.
