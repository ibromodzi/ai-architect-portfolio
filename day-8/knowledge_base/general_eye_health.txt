General Eye Health and Hygiene

The eyes are complex and delicate organs. Protecting them through everyday habits and regular professional care is one of the most effective ways to maintain good vision throughout life. Many of the leading causes of visual impairment globally are preventable or treatable when identified early through routine eye examinations.

Adults are generally recommended to have a comprehensive eye examination at least every two years, or more frequently if they have risk factors such as diabetes, a family history of eye disease, or existing eye conditions. Children should have their vision assessed as part of routine health checks, since conditions such as amblyopia (lazy eye), strabismus (squint), and refractive errors are more effectively treated the earlier they are identified. In many African health systems, eye screening is integrated into child health services and school programmes.

Protective habits that support eye health include wearing sunglasses that block ultraviolet A and B (UVA and UVB) radiation, especially in bright outdoor conditions. UV exposure over time is associated with cataract development and damage to the retina. Eye protection should also be worn during activities involving dust, chemicals, tools, or sport where there is a risk of eye injury. At work or school, following occupational health guidelines for screen use — including taking regular short breaks and ensuring adequate lighting — can reduce eye strain.

Nutrition contributes to eye health. Diets rich in leafy green vegetables, colourful fruits, and foods containing omega-3 fatty acids (such as fish) provide nutrients including lutein, zeaxanthin, and vitamins C and E that are associated with a lower risk of age-related eye conditions. Adequate hydration also supports the tear film that keeps the eye surface comfortable.

Contact lens users should follow hygiene instructions carefully: washing hands before handling lenses, using the correct lens solutions, not wearing lenses longer than recommended, and avoiding sleeping in lenses unless specifically prescribed for overnight wear. Improper contact lens use is a common cause of preventable eye infections.

Smoking is a significant risk factor for several serious eye conditions, including age-related macular degeneration, cataracts, and diabetic retinopathy. Stopping smoking at any age reduces the risk of these conditions developing or worsening.
