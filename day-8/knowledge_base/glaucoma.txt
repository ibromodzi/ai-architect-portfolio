Glaucoma

Glaucoma is a group of eye conditions that damage the optic nerve, which carries visual information from the eye to the brain. It is a leading cause of irreversible blindness globally and disproportionately affects people of African descent, who have a higher lifetime risk and tend to develop the condition at a younger age. Most forms of glaucoma are associated with elevated intraocular pressure (IOP), though the condition can occur at normal pressure levels as well.

The most common form is primary open-angle glaucoma, which develops slowly and without pain. In its early stages, it typically causes no noticeable symptoms because peripheral (side) vision is affected first, and the brain compensates for the loss. By the time central vision is affected, significant and irreversible damage may already have occurred. This is why glaucoma is often called the "silent thief of sight." Angle-closure glaucoma, a less common but more acute form, can cause sudden eye pain, headache, nausea, blurred vision, and halos around lights and requires urgent medical attention.

Risk factors for glaucoma include increasing age, elevated intraocular pressure, a family history of glaucoma, African or Hispanic ethnicity, previous eye injury, prolonged use of corticosteroid medications, and certain medical conditions such as diabetes and hypertension. Regular eye examinations that include measurement of intraocular pressure and assessment of the optic nerve are the primary way to detect glaucoma before significant vision loss occurs.

Although glaucoma cannot be cured and any vision already lost cannot be restored, progression can be slowed or halted with appropriate management. Treatment options include prescription eye drops to reduce intraocular pressure, laser procedures, and surgery. Adherence to prescribed treatment is essential, as glaucoma is a lifelong condition requiring ongoing monitoring.

Routine eye examinations are especially important for individuals with risk factors. People with a family history of glaucoma, those over 40, and individuals with known elevated eye pressure should discuss the frequency of screening with an eye care professional.
