Diabetic Retinopathy

Diabetic retinopathy is a diabetes-related eye complication that affects the blood vessels of the retina — the light-sensitive tissue at the back of the eye. It is a leading cause of vision loss and blindness among adults of working age globally. The condition develops when prolonged high blood sugar levels damage the small blood vessels that supply the retina, causing them to leak, swell, or grow abnormally.

Diabetic retinopathy progresses through two main stages. In the earlier stage, known as non-proliferative diabetic retinopathy (NPDR), the walls of the retinal blood vessels weaken and may leak fluid or blood into the retina. This can cause swelling in the central part of the retina (the macula), a condition called diabetic macular oedema (DMO), which affects central and detailed vision. In the more advanced stage, proliferative diabetic retinopathy (PDR), new and fragile blood vessels grow abnormally on the surface of the retina. These vessels bleed easily and can lead to scar tissue formation, which may pull on the retina and cause retinal detachment.

In the early stages, diabetic retinopathy often produces no symptoms or only mild changes in vision that can be easily overlooked. Symptoms that may appear as the condition advances include blurred or fluctuating vision, dark spots or floaters in the field of vision, difficulty seeing colours, and sudden vision loss. Because early-stage disease is often asymptomatic, people with diabetes are recommended to have regular dilated eye examinations — at least once a year — regardless of whether they notice any vision changes.

Good management of diabetes is the most important factor in preventing or slowing the progression of diabetic retinopathy. Maintaining blood sugar, blood pressure, and blood cholesterol within recommended ranges significantly reduces the risk. Avoiding smoking also lowers risk. When detected early, treatment options including laser therapy and injections into the eye can help prevent severe vision loss, though they do not cure the underlying condition.

People with diabetes who notice any sudden change in their vision should seek prompt assessment from an eye care professional. Regular screening is essential, as early intervention is far more effective than treating advanced disease.
