Cataracts

A cataract is a clouding of the eye's natural lens, which sits behind the iris and pupil. The lens focuses light onto the retina to produce clear images. When the lens becomes cloudy, light cannot pass through as easily, and vision becomes blurred or hazy. Cataracts are the leading cause of blindness worldwide and account for a significant proportion of visual impairment in Africa, where access to surgical treatment remains limited in many areas.

Cataracts develop gradually and are most commonly associated with aging. The proteins in the lens can clump together over time, forming cloudy areas that interfere with clear vision. Risk factors include advancing age, prolonged exposure to ultraviolet (UV) sunlight, smoking, excessive alcohol consumption, diabetes, long-term use of corticosteroid medications, and previous eye injury or surgery. Cataracts can also be present at birth (congenital cataracts) or develop in childhood due to infection, injury, or metabolic conditions.

Common symptoms of cataracts include blurred or cloudy vision, fading or yellowing of colours, difficulty with night vision, increased sensitivity to glare from lights, and seeing halos around lights. Double vision in one eye and frequent changes to eyeglass or contact lens prescriptions may also occur. In the early stages, stronger lighting and updated glasses may help manage symptoms. However, as the cataract progresses, these measures become less effective.

The only effective treatment for cataracts is surgery, which involves removing the cloudy lens and replacing it with a clear artificial lens. Cataract surgery is one of the most commonly performed and successful surgical procedures globally. It is typically an outpatient procedure and, where available, has a high rate of restoring useful vision. In many lower-income settings, community eye health programmes work to increase access to cataract surgery as a public health intervention.

Protecting the eyes from UV radiation by wearing sunglasses and broad-brimmed hats, avoiding smoking, and managing conditions such as diabetes can help reduce the risk of developing cataracts earlier in life.
