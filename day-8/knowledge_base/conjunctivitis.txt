Conjunctivitis (Pink Eye)

Conjunctivitis, commonly called pink eye, is an inflammation or infection of the conjunctiva — the thin, transparent layer of tissue that lines the inner surface of the eyelid and covers the white part of the eye. It is one of the most common eye conditions worldwide and can affect people of all ages.

There are three main types of conjunctivitis. Viral conjunctivitis is the most common form and is often associated with the common cold or upper respiratory infections. It typically causes a watery or mucous discharge, redness, and a gritty feeling in one or both eyes. Bacterial conjunctivitis produces a thicker, yellowish-green discharge that may cause the eyelids to stick together, especially after sleep. Allergic conjunctivitis is triggered by allergens such as pollen, dust mites, or animal dander, and usually affects both eyes simultaneously, causing intense itching, tearing, and swelling.

Common symptoms of conjunctivitis include redness in the white of the eye, increased tearing, a discharge that may form a crust during sleep, itching or burning sensation, and a feeling that something is in the eye. In most cases, conjunctivitis does not affect vision. However, light sensitivity may occur with some forms of the condition.

Conjunctivitis caused by viruses or bacteria can be highly contagious and spreads through direct or indirect contact with the secretions of an infected person. Good hygiene practices, such as frequent handwashing, avoiding touching the eyes, and not sharing towels or pillowcases, are important in preventing the spread of infectious conjunctivitis. Allergic conjunctivitis is not contagious.

Most cases of conjunctivitis resolve on their own within one to two weeks without specific treatment. Applying a clean, warm or cool compress to closed eyes may help relieve discomfort. Anyone experiencing significant pain, sensitivity to light, blurred vision, or symptoms that worsen or do not improve should seek assessment from a healthcare professional.
